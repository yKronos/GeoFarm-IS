# Synthesis

The reviewed literature and studies collectively establish a strong foundation for the development of GeoFarm-IS as an integrated agricultural management system for the Municipality of Tumauini, Isabela. The synthesis of these works reveals four interconnected themes that directly inform the system's design, implementation, and expected outcomes.

## Convergence of Digital Transformation in Agricultural Management

The literature demonstrates a clear consensus on the necessity and feasibility of digitizing agricultural information systems at the local government level. The works of Delos Reyes and Sanchez (2022), Santos et al. (2023), and Rivera et al. (2023) provide empirical evidence that web-based farmer registry systems significantly improve data accuracy, reduce processing time, and enhance access to government programs. These findings validate the core premise of GeoFarm-IS: that transitioning from manual, spreadsheet-based records to a centralized digital platform will yield measurable improvements in operational efficiency for the Tumauini Agriculture Office.

Notably, the integration of QR code technology, as demonstrated by Rivera et al. (2023) and Reyes et al. (2020), emerges as a practical innovation that bridges digital records with field-level transactions. This feature addresses a critical gap in existing systems where farmer identification during field visits and program distribution remains cumbersome. GeoFarm-IS incorporates this proven technology to streamline farmer verification processes.

## Spatial Intelligence Through GIS Integration

The application of Geographic Information Systems in agricultural monitoring represents a mature area of research with direct applicability to municipal-level operations. The foundational work of Longley et al. (2022) provides the theoretical underpinning for spatial analysis, while the regional study by Sarmiento et al. (2022) demonstrates that GIS tools are not only viable but highly relevant at the provincial and municipal scale in Isabela Province specifically.

The technical guidance provided by Corpuz and Lontoc (2021) regarding open-source mapping libraries (Leaflet.js and OpenLayers) directly informed the technology selection for GeoFarm-IS, ensuring that the system remains cost-effective and sustainable for resource-constrained LGU environments. De la Cruz and Ramos (2023) further validated the GPS-based coordinate collection methodology that will be employed during the system's data gathering phase. Together, these studies confirm that GIS integration is both technically feasible and operationally valuable for visualizing farmer land locations, crop distribution, and livestock density—core features of the GeoFarm-IS mapping module.

## Predictive Analytics as an Early Warning Mechanism

The emerging body of work on predictive analytics in agriculture reveals a pragmatic approach to risk management that aligns with the capabilities and constraints of Philippine LGUs. Dela Peña et al. (2022) and Garcia and Santos (2021) demonstrated that statistical trend analysis and threshold-based models can effectively identify farmers at risk of financial losses using historical agricultural data. This finding is particularly significant for GeoFarm-IS, as it validates the use of simpler, interpretable models over complex machine learning algorithms—an approach recommended by the World Bank (2022) for developing country contexts.

Lopez et al. (2023) reinforced that even basic regression and rule-based systems can achieve actionable predictive accuracy when trained on local data. This evidence supports the design decision to implement a threshold-based "palugi" (loss) prediction module in GeoFarm-IS that analyzes yield-to-input cost ratios across consecutive seasons. The predictive analytics component thus represents a practical, evidence-based intervention tool rather than an experimental feature.

## Agile Methodology as an Enabler of Iterative Development

The adoption of Agile software development methodology, particularly the Scrum framework, is well-supported by both Pressman (2022) and Sommerville (2021). These authoritative sources establish that iterative, feedback-driven development is particularly suited for projects where end-user requirements evolve during implementation—a characteristic inherent to GeoFarm-IS given the ongoing consultations with the Tumauini Agriculture Office.

The structured sprint-based approach proposed for GeoFarm-IS aligns with recognized best practices for incremental delivery, continuous integration, and stakeholder engagement. This methodology ensures that each module (Farmer Registry, GIS Mapping, Predictive Analytics, Assistance Tracking) can be developed, tested, and refined independently while maintaining system-wide coherence.

## Research Gaps and Contribution of GeoFarm-IS

While the reviewed literature provides strong support for individual components of agricultural information systems, a notable gap exists in the integration of these components into a unified platform specifically designed for Philippine municipal agriculture offices. Existing studies have examined farmer registries, GIS mapping, and predictive analytics as separate domains, but few have demonstrated their convergence into a single, cohesive system that addresses the full spectrum of agricultural management needs at the LGU level.

GeoFarm-IS addresses this gap by synthesizing proven technologies and methodologies into an integrated platform that combines:
- **Centralized farmer profiling** with RSBSA compliance and QR code integration
- **Geospatial visualization** of agricultural resources using open-source GIS tools
- **Predictive analytics** for early identification of at-risk farmers
- **Seasonal tracking** of crop production and livestock inventory
- **Assistance program management** with distribution monitoring

Furthermore, while previous studies have focused on provincial or regional implementations, GeoFarm-IS is specifically tailored to the operational context, resource constraints, and data management needs of a single municipality (Tumauini, Isabela). This localized approach allows for deeper customization and more direct stakeholder engagement than broader-scale systems.

## Theoretical and Practical Implications

The reviewed literature establishes that GeoFarm-IS is not merely a technological innovation but a theoretically grounded intervention supported by empirical evidence from similar contexts. The system's design reflects best practices in digital transformation (Delos Reyes & Sanchez, 2022), spatial analysis (Longley et al., 2022), predictive modeling (Dela Peña et al., 2022), and agile development (Pressman, 2022).

Practically, the synthesis of these works suggests that GeoFarm-IS has strong potential to achieve its stated objectives: improving data accuracy, enabling spatial monitoring, providing early warning for farmer financial distress, and streamlining assistance program delivery. The convergence of evidence from Philippine-specific studies (Santos et al., 2023; Sarmiento et al., 2022; De la Cruz & Ramos, 2023) further strengthens confidence in the system's contextual appropriateness and likelihood of successful adoption.

## Conclusion

The reviewed literature and studies provide a robust foundation for the development and implementation of GeoFarm-IS. The convergence of evidence across four thematic areas—digital farmer registries, GIS integration, predictive analytics, and agile methodology—validates the system's design approach and expected outcomes. By addressing the identified research gap of integrated agricultural management systems at the municipal level, GeoFarm-IS represents both a practical solution to operational challenges faced by the Tumauini Agriculture Office and a contribution to the broader body of knowledge on digital transformation in Philippine local government agricultural services.
