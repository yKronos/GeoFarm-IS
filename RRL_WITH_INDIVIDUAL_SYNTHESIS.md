# Review of Related Literature and Studies

A growing body of research supports the integration of information technology into agricultural management at the local government level. This chapter synthesizes relevant literature and studies under thematic areas that directly inform the development of GeoFarm-IS.

## Agricultural Information Systems and Farmer Registries

The digitization of farmer records has become a priority across developing economies. Delos Reyes and Sanchez (2022) demonstrated the feasibility of web-based farmer registry systems for Local Government Units in the Philippines, highlighting significant reductions in processing time and data inconsistency when digital platforms replaced manual forms. Similarly, Santos et al. (2023) found that the implementation of RSBSA-compliant digital registration in selected Philippine LGUs resulted in more accurate farmer profiling and improved access to government agricultural programs. These findings directly support the rationale for transitioning the Municipality of Tumauini from spreadsheet-based records to a centralized digital registry.

Rivera et al. (2023) specifically examined the design of farmer profiling systems for municipal agriculture offices and identified QR code integration as a high-impact feature for streamlining field transactions. Reyes et al. (2020) provided empirical evidence that QR code-based farmer identification systems significantly reduced transaction time in government service delivery. These studies collectively validate the core Farmer Registry and QR Code modules of GeoFarm-IS.

**Synthesis.** The reviewed studies on agricultural information systems converge on a clear finding: digital farmer registries significantly outperform manual systems in terms of data accuracy, processing efficiency, and program accessibility. The consistent evidence from Philippine-specific contexts (Delos Reyes & Sanchez, 2022; Santos et al., 2023; Rivera et al., 2023) validates the transition from spreadsheet-based records to a centralized web-based platform for Tumauini. The integration of QR code technology, supported by empirical evidence from Reyes et al. (2020), addresses a practical need for rapid farmer identification during field operations and assistance distribution. These works collectively establish the technical feasibility and operational value of the Farmer Registry module in GeoFarm-IS.

## Geographic Information Systems in Agriculture

The application of GIS technology in agricultural monitoring is well-established in literature. Longley et al. (2022) provided foundational principles of geographic information science and systems that underpin spatial analysis methods employed in GeoFarm-IS. Sarmiento et al. (2022) conducted geospatial analysis of crop distribution and productivity in Isabela Province specifically, demonstrating the applicability of GIS tools at the provincial and municipal scale in the region.

Corpuz and Lontoc (2021) developed a web-based GIS platform for agricultural resource management in Philippine LGUs, reporting that Leaflet.js and OpenLayers are optimal open-source mapping libraries for resource-constrained environments such as municipal offices. Their findings directly informed the GIS technology stack selection for GeoFarm-IS. De la Cruz and Ramos (2023) further established best practices for GIS-based mapping of agricultural land use in the Philippines, including GPS-based coordinate collection methods that inform the data collection approach for GeoFarm-IS's mapping module.

**Synthesis.** The literature on GIS applications in agriculture demonstrates both theoretical maturity and practical applicability at the municipal level. Longley et al. (2022) provide the conceptual foundation, while Sarmiento et al. (2022) confirm regional relevance in Isabela Province. The technical guidance from Corpuz and Lontoc (2021) regarding open-source mapping libraries directly addresses the resource constraints typical of Philippine LGUs, making GIS integration both feasible and sustainable. De la Cruz and Ramos (2023) validate the GPS-based data collection methodology that will be employed in GeoFarm-IS. Together, these studies establish that spatial visualization of farmer land locations, crop distribution, and livestock density is not only technically achievable but operationally valuable for agricultural planning and monitoring at the municipal scale.

## Predictive Analytics in Agriculture

Predictive analytics in agriculture has emerged as a critical tool for early warning and risk management. Dela Peña et al. (2022) developed a farmer risk prediction model using historical agricultural data in the Philippine context, demonstrating that statistical trend analysis and threshold-based alerts are viable methods for identifying farmers susceptible to financial losses—validating the initial methodology chosen for GeoFarm-IS's predictive module. Garcia and Santos (2021) applied data mining techniques to identify at-risk farmers in government agricultural assistance programs, identifying key indicators such as declining yield-to-input cost ratios across consecutive seasons.

Lopez et al. (2023) examined machine learning approaches for predicting crop losses due to climate variability, finding that even simpler regression and threshold models can achieve actionable predictive accuracy when trained on local historical data. The World Bank (2022) reinforced the value of predictive analytics specifically for agricultural resilience in developing countries, recommending rule-based systems as practical entry points before more complex ML models are deployed—an approach reflected in GeoFarm-IS's architecture design.

**Synthesis.** The body of work on predictive analytics reveals a pragmatic consensus: simpler, interpretable models are more appropriate than complex machine learning algorithms for resource-constrained LGU contexts. Dela Peña et al. (2022) and Garcia and Santos (2021) provide Philippine-specific evidence that threshold-based models using yield-to-input cost ratios can effectively identify farmers at financial risk. Lopez et al. (2023) reinforce that local historical data, even with basic statistical methods, can yield actionable predictions. The World Bank's (2022) recommendation for rule-based systems as practical entry points validates the design decision to implement a threshold-based "palugi" prediction module in GeoFarm-IS. These studies collectively support predictive analytics not as an experimental feature but as an evidence-based early warning mechanism for farmer financial distress.

## Agile Software Development Methodology

The Scrum framework within the Agile SDLC has been widely adopted for iterative software development projects in academic and professional settings. Pressman (2022) outlined the principles of agile development that enable rapid, feedback-driven iteration suitable for systems where end-user requirements evolve during development—a characteristic of the GeoFarm-IS project given the ongoing consultations with the Tumauini Agriculture Office. Sommerville (2021) further elaborated on agile practices including sprint planning, daily stand-ups, and sprint reviews as mechanisms for quality assurance and stakeholder engagement.

The seven-sprint development structure proposed for GeoFarm-IS aligns with recognized agile best practices that ensure incremental delivery of functional modules, continuous integration, and structured testing at each phase of development.

**Synthesis.** The literature on Agile software development methodology, particularly the works of Pressman (2022) and Sommerville (2021), establishes a strong theoretical foundation for the iterative development approach adopted in GeoFarm-IS. Both authors emphasize that Agile methodologies are particularly suited for projects with evolving requirements and active stakeholder involvement—characteristics inherent to the GeoFarm-IS development process. The sprint-based structure, continuous integration practices, and iterative testing cycles proposed for GeoFarm-IS directly reflect the best practices outlined in these authoritative sources. This methodological alignment ensures that the system can be developed incrementally, tested continuously, and refined based on user feedback, thereby increasing the likelihood of successful adoption and long-term sustainability.
